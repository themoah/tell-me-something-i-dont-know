Here’s a genuinely mind-blowing fact most people don’t know—and it’s about something you likely interact with daily:

Bananas are slightly radioactive. Not dangerously so—you’d need to eat ~10 million bananas at once to get a lethal dose—but they contain naturally occurring potassium-40 (a radioactive isotope of potassium). Since bananas are famously high in potassium, they emit a tiny amount of radiation. Scientists even use this as a informal unit called the "Banana Equivalent Dose" (BED) to explain low-level radiation risks (e.g., a chest X-ray is about 100 BED).

Why this is surprising:

  • We associate radioactivity with nuclear plants or medical treatments—not breakfast fruit.
  • The radiation is so low it’s harmless (your body contains potassium-40 too!), but it’s measurable with a Geiger counter.
  • It highlights how "radioactive" doesn’t automatically mean "dangerous"—context and dose matter immensely.

Bonus depth:

This same potassium-40 is why human bones are faintly radioactive (we rebuild them constantly using dietary potassium). Even the Earth’s crust emits radiation from isotopes like uranium and thorium—so we’re all living in a low-level radioactive bath… and it’s totally normal.
