You can hear with your eyes.

There's a phenomenon called the McGurk Effect that proves your brain doesn't just listen to sound—it watches faces. When you see someone speak, your visual cortex overrides your auditory processing, even when you know exactly what's being said.

Here's how it works: If you watch a video of someone silently mouthing the word "fafa" while the audio plays the sound "baba," you won't hear either correctly. Instead, your brain manufactures a compromise: you'll swear you heard "vava" or "thatha." Your eyes convince your ears they heard something that was never actually said.

The wild part? Even after you learn about the effect, watch the video a dozen times, and consciously focus on the actual audio, you still can't hear the real sound. The illusion is unbreakable because it's happening below conscious thought—your brain is stitching together sensory inputs before you have a say in the matter.

It was discovered by accident in 1976 when Harry McGurk asked his lab technician to dub a video, and the result revealed that human speech perception isn't just hearing plus lip-reading added together. It's a complete sensory fusion where vision often dominates. You don't just hear voices; you see them.
