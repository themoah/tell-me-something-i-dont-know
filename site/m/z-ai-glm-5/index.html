In the 18th century, wealthy landowners in England took the trend of landscaping to a bizarre extreme by hiring "garden hermits."

These were real people hired to live in secluded huts, caves, or follies on the estate. They were typically contracted to act the part of a wise hermit—refusing to cut their hair or nails, wearing ragged robes, and remaining mostly silent—for a set period of years.

It was essentially a form of living theater. Having a "philosophical hermit" on your property was considered a high-status conversation piece for guests, meant to evoke a sense of ancient wisdom and melancholy. Some hermits were even contractually forbidden from leaving the property or cleaning themselves to maintain the illusion.

The national animal of Scotland is the Unicorn.

While it might seem like a mythical creature belongs in a fantasy novel rather than a country's official heraldry, the unicorn has been a Scottish heraldic symbol since the 12th century. It was chosen because, in ancient mythology, the unicorn was seen as the natural enemy of the lion (which is the national animal of England), a symbolism that goes back to a time when the two nations were fierce rivals.

Today, you can see the unicorn on the Royal Coat of Arms of the United Kingdom, where it is chained. In mythology, the unicorn was considered a wild, untamable beast, so the chains represent the immense power required to subdue it.
