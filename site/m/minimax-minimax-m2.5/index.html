One thing that most people don’t hear about is the Oklo Natural Nuclear Reactor—the only known example of a self‑sustaining, ancient nuclear fission reactor that occurred naturally on Earth.

  • Where & when: In the region of Oklo, near the town of Franceville in present‑day Gabon (West Africa), about 2 billion years ago.
  • How it happened: The uranium ore at Oklo was enriched enough (about 3 % U‑235, compared with the ~0.7 % natural abundance today) that, when groundwater seeped in, it acted as a neutron moderator, allowing a chain reaction to start.
  • Duration: The reactor “went critical” and operated intermittently for several hundred thousand years, producing a steady output of about 100 kW of thermal power.
  • Evidence: Scientists discovered the site in 1972, when analysis of the ore showed a slight depletion of U‑235 and the presence of fission products (e.g., xenon, neodymium isotopes) that matched the expected signatures of a nuclear reactor.

The Oklo reactors are a striking reminder that the conditions for nuclear energy can arise naturally under the right geological circumstances—something that few people realize when they think of “natural” nuclear phenomena. It also offers a unique case study for geologists and nuclear engineers interested in long‑term waste containment, because the reactor’s by‑products have remained relatively contained for billions of years.

Here’s a little‑known fact that might surprise you:

There is a natural nuclear reactor in the world.
In the 1970s geologists discovered that a uranium‑rich ore deposit in Oklo, Gabon, underwent sustained nuclear fission about two billion years ago. The site—now called the Oklo Reactor—is the only known natural nuclear fission reactor on Earth.

How it worked: At that time the concentration of the fissile isotope uranium‑235 in natural uranium was higher than today (about 3 % vs. the current 0.7 %). When a sufficiently large pocket of uranium‑rich rock became saturated with groundwater, the water acted as a neutron moderator, allowing a chain reaction to proceed. The reaction lasted for a few hundred thousand years, producing heat and leaving behind a distinctive pattern of fission products that we can still detect today.

So, long before humans built the first artificial nuclear reactors, nature already had one running deep underground. It’s a striking reminder that “natural” and “nuclear” aren’t mutually exclusive.
