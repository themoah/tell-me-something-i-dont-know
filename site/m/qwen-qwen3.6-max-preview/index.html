That discrepancy led to a stunning conclusion: self-sustaining nuclear fission had occurred there, naturally, long before humans existed.

How it worked:

  • Higher U-235 abundance: Because U-235 decays faster than U-238, natural uranium contained ~3% U-235 two billion years ago (close to the enrichment level used in modern light-water reactors).
  • Groundwater as a moderator: Rainwater seeped into the uranium-rich sandstone, slowing neutrons just enough to trigger a chain reaction.
  • A geological thermostat: When the reaction heated up, the water boiled away, stopping the fission. Once the rock cooled, water returned, and the cycle repeated. Each "reactor zone" pulsed on and off for hundreds of thousands of years, averaging about 100 kilowatts of power (enough to run a few dozen homes).

Why it matters:

  • The fission byproducts (like specific isotopes of neodymium, ruthenium, and xenon) match exactly what you'd expect from a nuclear reactor, confirming the phenomenon beyond doubt.
  • Remarkably, the radioactive waste stayed almost perfectly contained in the surrounding rock for two billion years. Nuclear engineers still study Oklo to understand how to safely store modern spent fuel over geological timescales.
  • Physicists have even used Oklo's isotopic signatures to test whether fundamental constants of nature (like the fine-structure constant) have changed over billions of years. So far, they haven't.

Most people haven't heard of Oklo because it sits at the intersection of geology, nuclear physics, and deep time, and it doesn't fit neatly into standard curricula.
