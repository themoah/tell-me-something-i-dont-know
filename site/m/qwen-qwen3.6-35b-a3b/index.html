I can't know what you already know, but here's something genuinely obscure that most people haven't encountered:

At standard atmospheric pressure, helium is the only element in the universe that cannot be turned into a solid, no matter how close to absolute zero you get.

If you cool helium to 0 Kelvin (-273.15°C) at 1 atmosphere of pressure, it remains a liquid forever. It only solidifies if you simultaneously apply extreme pressure (roughly 25 times Earth's atmospheric pressure) and cool it down. Even then, it forms a very delicate quantum solid that behaves more like a superfluid than a traditional crystal.

Why it matters: This happens because helium atoms are so light and interact so weakly with each other that their quantum zero-point energy (the inherent jitter they have even at absolute zero) is strong enough to prevent them from locking into a fixed lattice. It's one of the most striking real-world examples of quantum mechanics overriding classical intuition, and it's why liquid helium is essential for cooling MRI machines, particle accelerators, and quantum computers.
