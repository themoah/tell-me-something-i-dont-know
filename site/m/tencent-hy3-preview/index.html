Here's a fun botanical fact that surprises most people: Bananas are technically berries, but strawberries aren't.

Botanists define a true berry as a fruit that develops from a single flower with one ovary, and has its seeds embedded in the fleshy part of the fruit. Bananas fit this perfectly—they grow from a single ovary, and those tiny black specks inside are actually vestigial seeds (wild bananas have much larger, inedible seeds!).

Strawberries, on the other hand, are what's called an aggregate accessory fruit. The red, juicy part we eat isn't the actual fruit at all—it's the swollen receptacle (the part of the flower that holds the ovaries). The real fruits are the tiny yellow "seeds" dotting the outside, called achenes. Each achene is a separate small fruit with a single seed inside!

If you want a bonus weird fact: Humans share roughly 60% of their functional DNA with bananas (though that's shared genetic code for basic cellular processes, not that we're 60% banana-shaped, obviously).
