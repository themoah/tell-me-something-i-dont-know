Why does it happen? It’s still a topic of active research, but a few leading explanations include:

  1. Evaporation – Hot water loses mass more quickly, so there’s less water left to freeze.
  2. Convection currents – As water cools, it creates circulating motions that help it lose heat more efficiently.
  3. Dissolved gases – Heating drives out dissolved gases (like oxygen and carbon dioxide), which can insulate the water and slow cooling.
  4. Supercooling suppression – Cold water can become “supercooled” (stay liquid below 0 °C) before suddenly snapping into ice; hot water is less prone to this.

The effect isn’t guaranteed—it depends on the exact conditions (container shape, temperature range, surrounding air, etc.)—but under the right circumstances, hot water will indeed beat cold water to the ice‑cube finish line.
