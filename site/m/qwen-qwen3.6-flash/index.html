Deep in a quiet forest in Gabon, Central Africa, there's a patch of land where nature built and operated nuclear reactors about 1.7 billion years ago—long before complex life even existed.

These weren't human-made. They're natural nuclear fission reactors. When Earth was that young, the radioactive isotope uranium-235 made up about 3% of natural uranium (similar to today's enriched reactor fuel). In certain ore deposits, groundwater seeped in and slowed down neutrons, triggering a sustained chain reaction. The heat would boil the water away, halting the reaction. Once cooled, water returned, and it would start again. This "on-off" cycling continued for hundreds of thousands of years.

When French scientists discovered the site in 1972, they initially thought uranium ore had been tampered with. Instead, they found geological proof that the Earth itself had run nuclear reactions. Today, researchers still study Oklo because it shows how radioactive byproducts can be safely locked away in rock formations for geological timescales. It's essentially a billion-year-old natural experiment that helps engineers design safer long-term nuclear waste repositories.

You'll never see a sign for it. It's just a forest in Gabon where plate tectonics, groundwater, and radioactive decay accidentally did what we're still trying to master.
