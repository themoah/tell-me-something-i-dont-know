Here’s something that’s well-established in neuroscience but rarely discussed outside academic circles:

Your brain doesn’t experience the world in real time. It’s running about 80–100 milliseconds behind reality.

When light hits your retina or a sound wave reaches your ear, it takes that fraction of a second for signals to travel through neural pathways and be assembled into a coherent percept. If you experienced raw sensory input exactly as it arrived, the world would feel laggy and disjointed. To compensate, your brain uses a process called predictive coding: instead of passively recording what’s happening, it constantly generates rapid, unconscious predictions about what’s about to occur, then compares those predictions with incoming sensory data. When they match, you experience smooth, seamless reality. When they don’t, you feel surprise, disorientation, or the urge to double-check what you just perceived.

This isn’t just theory. It’s been measured using neural timing experiments, explains perceptual quirks like the "flash-lag effect" (where a moving dot appears ahead of a synchronized flash), and underpins why you can catch a ball or step off a curb without consciously calculating physics. Your brain has already simulated the outcome.

The twist? This predictive system is why certain illusions, déjà vu, or even mild sensory mismatches (like VR motion sickness) happen: your brain’s internal model got confidently wrong, and reality had to force a quick recalibration.
