Your immune system doesn’t know your eyes exist.

And it’s a good thing they don't meet, because if they did, your immune system would likely attack your eyes and blind you.

Inside your eyes is a fluid called the aqueous humor. It creates a "blood-ocular barrier" that keeps immune cells out. Because of this, the proteins in your eyes are completely hidden from your white blood cells. Since your immune system never encounters these proteins during its early "training" phase, it never learns to recognize them as "self."

If you suffer a severe injury to one eye—like a puncture or trauma—that barrier can break. Immune cells finally get in, see the eye proteins, and think, “What is this strange invader? We must destroy it!”

The terrifying part is that once the immune system identifies the eye tissue as an enemy, it doesn't just attack the injured eye. It sends white blood cells through the bloodstream to attack the healthy eye as well, which can lead to total blindness. This rare but devastating condition is called sympathetic ophthalmia.

Historically, the only way doctors could save the patient's vision in the uninjured eye was by completely removing the injured eye before the immune system could figure out what was going on. Today, it can sometimes be managed with aggressive immunosuppressants, but the biological loophole remains: your eyes are hiding in plain sight from your own body's defense system.

Your immune system doesn't know your eyes exist.

And it’s a good thing they don't meet, because if they did, your immune system would attack your eyes and likely blind you.

Here is how that bizarre reality works:

Your eyes have what is called "immune privilege." Because vision is so critical to survival, and because inflammation inside the eye would cause catastrophic damage to the delicate tissues required for sight, your eyes are walled off from the rest of your body's immune system. They have their own localized immune suppression system, and the white blood cells in the rest of your body are completely unaware of the specific proteins that make up your eyes.

Usually, this works out perfectly. But if you suffer a severe, penetrating injury to one eye—say, from a sharp object or a piece of metal—the barrier breaks. Eye proteins that have been locked away your entire life suddenly spill into your bloodstream.

When your immune system encounters these proteins for the first time, it doesn't recognize them. It assumes they are a foreign, invading pathogen. It creates antibodies to destroy this "invader."

The terrifying result is a condition called Sympathetic Ophthalmia. The immune system seeks out the eye proteins, attacks the injured eye, and destroys it. But because the immune system is now circulating throughout your whole body, those antibodies also travel to your healthy eye, which is made of the exact same proteins. The immune system attacks and destroys the healthy eye, too, leaving the person completely blind.

Historically, the only way doctors could save the healthy eye was to completely remove the injured eye before the immune system could mount its attack—a surgical procedure called enucleation. Today, it can sometimes be treated with heavy immunosuppressants, but it remains an incredibly rare and dangerous condition.

So, your eyes are essentially living in a witness protection program, hiding from your own immune system. If they are ever discovered, the consequences are devastating.
