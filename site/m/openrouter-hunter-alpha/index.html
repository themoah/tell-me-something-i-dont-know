Here's one that tends to surprise people:

Humans can smell rain better than sharks can smell blood.

The distinctive earthy scent that rises when rain hits dry soil — called petrichor — is largely caused by a molecule called geosmin, produced by soil bacteria. Humans can detect geosmin at concentrations as low as 5 parts per trillion. For comparison, sharks detect blood at roughly 1 part per million.

So you're about 200 times more sensitive to the smell of approaching rain than a shark is to its most famous target.

Nobody's entirely sure why we're so attuned to it, but one leading theory is that our ancestors evolved to associate that smell with water sources and fertile ground — essentially, survival. Which means every time you step outside after a storm and take that deep, satisfying breath, you're experiencing a tiny echo of something your brain has been wired to chase for hundreds of thousands of years.

Here's one that tends to surprise people:

There's a jellyfish called Turritopsis dohrnii that is effectively biologically immortal. When it reaches the end of its natural lifespan or gets injured, it can revert its cells back to an earlier developmental stage — essentially transforming itself from an adult back into a juvenile polyp — and then grow up again.
