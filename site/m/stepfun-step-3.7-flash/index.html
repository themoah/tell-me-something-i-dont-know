Here’s a little-known, surprisingly dark slice of U.S. history most people aren’t taught in standard classes: During Prohibition (1920-1933), the federal government intentionally poisoned industrial alcohol to stop people from drinking it, and this policy killed an estimated 10,000 to 15,000 people—more than all the gangland shootings and Prohibition-related violence combined.

For context: Prohibition banned the sale of drinkable alcohol, but industrial alcohol (used for things like solvents and cleaning products) was still legal, as long as it was "denatured" with mild bitterants and toxins to make it undrinkable. In the mid-1920s, the Prohibition Bureau ordered bootleg suppliers to add far more lethal poisons—including methanol, kerosene, and iodine—to industrial alcohol stockpiles, explicitly to make stolen booze deadly if sold to consumers.

They knew bootleggers would try to redistill the alcohol to remove impurities, but methanol was nearly impossible to fully filter out, and thousands of people who drank illegally sold alcohol died of poisoning, blindness, and organ failure as a result. The policy was so widely condemned that it was eventually scaled back, but the death toll from the poisoned alcohol program is almost never mentioned in standard summaries of the Prohibition era.

If you’d prefer a lighter obscure fact: The only national flag in the world that is not a quadrilateral (rectangle, square, etc.) is Nepal’s, which is made of two stacked triangular shapes.
